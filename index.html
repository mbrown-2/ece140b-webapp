Have you ever...

Gardening shouldn't be a pain, right?

Did I water my plants one too few or many times this past week? Perhaps, the soil itself isn't absorbant enough? Am I wasting too much water? Otherwise, could the atmosphere not be the right temperature?

Don't worry, we've all been there.

Whether you're a new gardener or experienced plant-caretaker, anyone is prone to at least one of these troubles. But alas, modern problems require modern solutions, and our simple, affordable solution that's just right for you!

Product Vision

Introducing...

PlantPal is the solution to your plant-caretaking woes. Our product consists of a packaged set of small meters that are inserted in the soil near the base of plants that you wish to track. These meters come with pre-installed batteries as well as mini solar panels to achieve energy sustainability for both indoor and outdoor environments.

PlantPal is partnered with a free, downloadable app that centralizes and simplifies your experience with our product. This app will contain several features, such as a "plant profiling" system in which users define the type of plant that each unique meter is measuring. Based on these descriptions, the PlantPal meters will adapt their rate of reminders to produce more-specialized results.

Product Details

The goal of our team is to create and introduce a product that will minimize the stress of plant-caretaking and help users improve their gardening awareness. PlantPal is a device two key integrations: a hardware and software side of things.

The Hardware: Each meter of our PlantPal package comes with a central raspberry pi, small rechargeable battery, (all tucked within a protective shell/case) and an extending meter. This meter sticks into the soil of a nearby plant and periodically obtains data. This data includes measurements of important factors such as soil moisture, temperature, and humidity. Every 30 minutes, data is forwarded via wifi to a remote server that will collect and store our data in an SQL database.

The software: The data is aimed to be hosted on Microsoft Azure for security and reliability. As data is collected, a user will be able to check on this information via an iOS application on their mobile device. This app will retrieve data and display information in a clean and graphical layout. Some implementations you can expect are charts that visually describe a plant's conditions over time and custom settings that allow you to describe the breed and dimensions of the plants you are taking care of.

Our product is especially built to minimize its impact upon the environment around. Our Key Value Proposition (KVP)

  1. Risk Reduction: PlantPal's minimum-energy usage through rest-mode cycles will save battery to preserve its lifespan and minimize heat temperatures to prevent damaging nearby plants. Hence, all important internals will be contained within protective shells to protect the device from natural weather events.
    2. convenience, and connectivity
  2. Accessibility: As mentioned earlier, PlantPal's price goal is $10 for a pack of 5 meters. These packs can also be purchased in several bulk counts for users who have larger gardens that others. Otherwise, this reasonable price should be obtainable for anyone who is interested. In addition, users who have access to any Apple devices will be able to use our partnered mobile app to its fullest capabilities.
  3. Convenience/Connectivity: The PlantPal app can be accessed on the go as long as you have access to an internet connection. The meters themselves, on the other hand, are easy to turn on and insert into the soil of nearby plants. Once inserted and paired with our app, the meters will request permission to connect to one's wifi. From there, measurements will be automated and stored for a user to access and check at any time they please.
Compared to other product competitors such as XLUX and ECOWITT meters are analog and do not feature customization. On the other hand, PlantPal simplifies interaction and condenses the data to one's device. Our competitors tend to have information displayed on the meters themselves; hence, PlantPal minimizes one's hassle and makes plant health easier to identify while on the go.

Wk. 5 - Principles of UI Design

Hick's Law

The first steering wheels for vehicles were - simply put - wheels. The only purpose required back then was to steer a car in a direction dependent upon the driver’s intent. On the other hand, vehicles of today are integrated with a variety of technologies such as comfortability options and screen displays. If we consider race cars instead of the average vehicle, we can see even more options such as shifting pedals and turn dials to maximize a vehicle’s performance.

Law of Proximity

As an example, we can consider the sections I organized on this website. At first glance, this idea wouldn’t seem appropriate for the law given that all of my work is on a single, continuous page. However, the personal design/goal is to create a streamline of similar ideas that carry the user from one topic to a new yet relevant topic and so forth, especially without the hassle of extra button navigation. In order to differentiate these ideas, I divide these sections into their own black-box dividers to proximize all text of a single idea from other sections.

Jacob's Law

For this example, we can consider a whole range of universal design choices that we take for granted. As for me, I thought it’d be interesting to consider the keyboard, the very device we use almost every day in our careers as programmers. The design of the keyboard is organized and grouped in the exact same layout across all versions. As users, we want and expect these systems to output the exact letter or symbol that we physically press. Otherwise, its implementation would be contradictory.

Wk. 6 - Product I.A.

User's First Interaction

As described in previous sections, the deliverable of this product will come in two parts: hardware (the set of meters) and software (the paired app on iOS). For a user's first interaction, they will need to know how to place the meters into soil. This should be extremely easy as one must simply push the spiked end of the meter into the ground and press its 'on' button to activate it.

On the other hand, we expect a user to feel comfortable with basic navigation of PlantPal's paired app as long as the user has interacted with basic phone applications before. One simply needs to open the app as they will then be greeted with a vertical navigation view of a list of paired meters. Each entry can be pressed to view the details of the plants they measure respectively.

The only possible difficulty one might face is the matter of pairing the meters to the device. As of now, our team speculates (and has proven) Bluetooth capability. In this scenario, users will have to access their settings to select the meters individually. Hopefully, we will be able to find a way to implement an automated feature through the app itself if time permits.

I.A. Design Principles

Principle of Objects: Every meter is paired with at least a single plant. Our team views the presence of our product's hardware as physical companions that watch over their live counterparts. A meter's role, in a sense, is to babysit a plant and provide necessary health updates to the users they assist. Through the app, each meter can show attributes of data (ie. moisture, temperature, and graphical comparisons).

Principle of Focused Navigation: In order to sort the content of our product's software, the iOS app will feature a simple tab-row at the bottom of the screen. As of now, we are focused on implementing three tabs: 'Overview', 'My Meters,' and 'Settings.' Each of these tabs should be self explanatory and contain data that is relevant to the tab's title they are sorted under. The 'Overview' will provide general summaries of a user's stats and notifications of current weather activity. The meters tab will feature a vertical list of paired meters while the settings tab will contain accessibility options, pairing features, and more.

Structural Principles

Sequential Navigation: So far, we've discussed our team's goals and working progress in the product's app implementation. Since we are focused on applying a tab-view and vertical navigation-view, we expect the app to function in a sequential pattern. For example: let's say the user seeks to view the tracked data of their PlantPal meter that is measuring a pot of sunflowers. A user will open the app and select the 'My Meters' tab to view their list of meters. They will then scroll through the list of names and select the meter that is measuring the said plant. Performing this action will transition the screen to a 'Details' page that presents data. In addition, we should mention that the user can also select the name of the meter in this Details page and edit the title so that it is more descriptive (hence why we assume in this example that the meter's name is something that describes the sunflower pot and is therefore recognizable from the user's perspective).

Chronological: We should also mention once again that our 'Details' page will feature an organized list of data and other recorded information. Given that our meters measure plants periodically, the data must be stored chronologically. This is especially prevalent for our team's goal to implement a graphical view (ie. a line graph that extends left to right over a course of time). This will help visualize a plant's rate of health as time goes on.
